In 2020, I opened a U.S. VEGA account with Cathay Bank, but the maintenance costs turned out to be so steep that I eventually closed it.

In 2021, I opened an account with Tiger Brokers.

Then in February 2025, I went on a spree — opening accounts with Futu, Longbridge, Interactive Brokers (IBKR), and a whole pile of Hong Kong bank cards along the way.

Truth is, I never had any real need to move large sums of money across borders. I've toyed with the idea of becoming a digital nomad, but never actually followed through. My life, my family, my income, my expenses — everything is rooted in mainland China, and I can't just walk away for now.

I have this quirk of mine: I like collecting accounts the way other people collect trading cards — all sorts of odd and obscure ones. As long as the barrier to entry isn't too high and I can set one up in fifteen or twenty minutes, I'll do it. To manage hundreds of accounts and their random passwords, I even bought a 1Password subscription. I also enjoy hoarding email domains with different suffixes and snapping up short domain names — including the one hosting this very blog, which cost me several thousand yuan and ranks as my second-most expensive writing investment.

But there's a real advantage to collecting early. When something is just taking off, the doors are wide open; once the crowds show up, the path slowly chokes shut. Like that student plan from China Telecom Macau — I casually signed up for one back then, never imagining I'd still be using it four or five years later without wanting to switch. Or those DMIT servers — you use them for a while and suddenly they're out of print. Or the Mac mini I bought not long ago, which has practically become a financial asset at this point.

Yesterday afternoon, after the market closed, I saw the news that the Chinese government was cracking down hard on cross-border stock trading and cross-border wealth management. My stomach dropped. I'm heavily leveraged into Xiaomi on margin through Futu, and I'm currently sitting on a floating loss of nearly 50%. If I'm restricted to selling only — no buying, no new deposits — I won't be able to day-trade my way out, and I won't survive a margin call if the stock keeps falling.

After calming down and thinking it through, the actual impact isn't as bad as it first seemed. **My biggest risk right now is really the margin risk**; if I weren't leveraged, I could easily transfer my positions elsewhere.

**First of all, buying stocks through Hong Kong local banks is completely unaffected.** This policy doesn't place any restrictions on stock purchases through banking channels. Take HSBC's Trade25, for example: pay a HK$25 monthly fee and you get HK$250,000 in commission-free trading. On top of that, banks like CMB Wing Lung Bank, ZA Bank, and Airstar all offer Hong Kong and U.S. stock trading services — the fees are a bit higher, sure, but they're still perfectly usable.

**Second, U.S.-based brokers are largely untouched.** My Interactive Brokers account is a U.S. account, not a Hong Kong one, so it's completely unaffected. In hindsight, that account suddenly feels like a bargain.

As for the safety of my funds, there's really no need to worry too much. Hong Kong's financial regulatory system is relatively robust — the risk of a platform just disappearing is practically zero.

Bottom line: the impact is real, but it's not catastrophic — no need to overthink it. I'm sitting on a floating loss with my margin position, so all I can do is ride it out. Two-year window, let's see what happens.
